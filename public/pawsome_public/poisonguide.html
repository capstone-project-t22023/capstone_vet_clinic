Poison Guide

Poison Guide


Welcome to the Poison Guide subpage of PawsomeVet Clinic. Your pet's safety is our utmost priority, and we understand that accidents can happen. This guide is designed to provide you with essential information about common household items, plants, and substances that can be toxic to your furry companions. In case of an emergency, always consult a veterinarian or call a poison control helpline immediately.





Common Household Poisons

  1. Human Medications: Keep all medications, including over-the-counter and prescription drugs, out of your pet's reach. Even a small dose of certain medications can be harmful. Pain relievers, antidepressants, and cold medicines are particularly dangerous.
  2. Household Cleaners: Cleaning products such as bleach, detergents, and disinfectants contain chemicals that can be harmful to pets if ingested or inhaled. Always store these products securely and ensure your pet's environment is well-ventilated when using them.
  3. Food Hazards: Some human foods are toxic to pets. Chocolate, grapes, raisins, onions, garlic, and certain artificial sweeteners (xylitol) can be extremely harmful. Keep these foods out of your pet's reach and be cautious about feeding table scraps.

Common Plant Poisons

  1. Lilies: Many species of lilies, especially those of the Lilium and Hemerocallis genera, are highly toxic to cats and can cause kidney failure even in small amounts.
  2. Sago Palm: This common indoor and outdoor plant contains toxins that can lead to liver failure in both dogs and cats if ingested.
  3. Autumn Crocus: Ingesting any part of this plant can cause severe gastrointestinal and kidney issues in pets.

Signs of Poisoning

If you suspect your pet has ingested a toxic substance, watch out for the following symptoms:

  • Vomiting or diarrhea
  • Lethargy or weakness
  • Excessive drooling
  • Tremors or seizures
  • Difficulty breathing
  • Changes in appetite or thirst
  • Abdominal pain
  • Changes in behavior

What to Do

  1. Stay Calm: It's important to remain composed. Panicking can make the situation more stressful for your pet.
  2. Contact a Veterinarian: Call your veterinarian or an emergency veterinary clinic immediately. If the incident occurs after hours, have the number of a 24-hour emergency vet on hand.
  3. Identify the Toxin: If possible, gather information about the substance your pet ingested. This will help the veterinarian determine the best course of action.
  4. Do Not Induce Vomiting: In some cases, inducing vomiting can worsen the situation. Always follow the guidance of a veterinary professional.
  5. Transport Safely: If instructed by the veterinarian, carefully transport your pet to the clinic. Use a carrier to ensure their safety during travel.

Prevention is Key

The best way to protect your beloved pets from poisoning is to prevent access to toxic substances. Store medications, cleaners, and hazardous materials in secure places, and keep plants that are toxic to pets out of reach. Additionally, educate yourself about potential dangers and always be prepared to act quickly in case of an emergency.
